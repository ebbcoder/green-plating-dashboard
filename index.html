Fleet Investment Intensity Ranking

2018–2025 additions divided by 2025 fleet size. A value above 50% means more than half the current fleet is less than 8 years old. The article calls this Averch-Johnson in action.

Fleet Growth Ranking — Additions vs 2018 Fleet

Same additions, different denominator: 2018–2025 additions divided by the 2018 owned fleet. This measures growth from the starting point rather than newness of the current fleet. A value of 50% means the utility added half-again as much capacity as it had in 2018 — i.e. its 2018 fleet plus 50% of that on top. Some Iowa and Wisconsin utilities have effectively doubled their owned fleets in eight years under this framing.

Sources: Net income: S&P Capital IQ (IQ_NET_INC_PARENT) for the 50 largest operating utility subsidiaries by cumulative 2018–2025 net income. Capacity: U.S. Energy Information Administration EIA-860 2024 final release. For each generator, nameplate MW is allocated to its owning utilities via the Ownership sheet (each owner gets MW × percent owned); jointly-owned plants are split across owners rather than credited entirely to the operator. For sole-owned plants the operator owns 100%. Intermediate holdcos (Berkshire Hathaway Energy, Progress Energy, Pepco Holdings, etc.), gas-only and transmission-only entities are excluded. Both additions and retirements come from the EIA-860 raw zip (sheets "Operable" + "Retired and Canceled"). The EIA API v2 doesn't expose retired generators, so the raw spreadsheet is the canonical source. Inspired by Green-Plating™ the Grid (Energy Bad Boys, March 2024).
